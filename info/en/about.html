About iDAI.objects arachne

iDAI.objects arachne (short form: Arachne) is the central object database of the German Archaeological Institute (DAI) and the Archaeological Institute of the University of Cologne. It is administered by Reinhard Förtsch and part of the iDAI.welt.

Arachne is an internet research tool for the Archaeologies and Altertumswissenschaft. It offers a means to access objects and their states and to search efficiently in hundreds of thousands of datasets. Arachne feeds from analogue and digital sources. Archival material is often not adequately documented and in danger of being destroyed over time; Arachne is actively digitizing and documenting these materials. On the other hand, there is an ever growing amount of born-digital images and object data. By employing Semantic Web strategies, Arachne provides a low-threshold structure for these data. All digitized images and textual data in Arachne are preserved for the long term and available online.

How to use

Arachne is provided free of charge. Creating an account is optional but recommended: images will be displayed at a higher resolution. In addition, you can create object catalogues and annotate the Arachne data.

In order to use Arachne you will need a modern internet browser. Recent version of Firefox, Chrome and Safari are supported. Modern versions of the Internet Explorer and Edge should also work. The browser needs to accept cookies and JavaScript. Normally these settings are active out of the box, but if you have problems displaying Arachne in your browser please make sure these settings are in fact active.

Arachne will be displayed in your preferred language if possible, with English as the default language. Since you see this text in English, your browser preference is either set to English or to another language for which no translation of this page exists at this time. Arachne uses the text encoding “Unicode (UTF-8)”. Your browser should recogonize this encoding automatically.

For optimal image display please use the calibration of Eye One for Gamma 2.2 and native white point. Licensing details and information about ordering images can be found here.

The concept behind Arachne

The database design reflects a world model based on two very simple assumptions in archaeology or art history: all objects in the “real world” should be comparable on a very general level, and all objects have a context. Thus Arachne attempts to avoid the mistakes of earlier databases whose project-specific object modellings led to small numbers of objects that were not comparable with each other and instead lived in their database silos. In contrast, all objects in Arachne have a common set of attributes in the object model. The common attributes are complemented by category-specific attributes, for example about the topography or architecture, to fully contextualize the objects. The common attributes can be queried over a large number of objects. On the other hand, more specific queries on the additional attributes will only find objects in their respective category.

Arachne aims to make different systems interoperable while at the same time preserving the copyright of the content creators. Wherever possible, the data is Open Access and the source code is Open Source. Arachne is a central part of the software architecture iDAI.welt of the DAI, which consists of several interconnected modules. The modules (e.g. Arachne itself, iDAI.field, iDAI.bibliography, iDAI.Gazetteer) are constantly being refined with the help of new technical and scientific methods and possibilities. Especially between Arachne as the central image database and the different geoinformation systems (GIS) used in excavations and surveys, interoperability is becoming ever more important for reducing redundancies in the data.

Arachne supports the Semantic Web by providing URIs for all digitally represented objects. Equally important are the connections to CIDOC CRM and the Open Archives Initiative. In addition, Arachne’s user interface is provided in several languages, currently English, German and Italian.

The DAI and the CoDArchLab have a policy of encouraging international cooperation to tackle the complex issues arising from this approach. International colloquia, the digital networking of the actors as well as courses at the CoDArchLab support the exchange of experiences and help scientists keep up to date with recent developments.

Technology

The name Arachne refers to the database itself as well as the system for displaying the data. The database is based on MariaDB, a free fork of MySQL. The long-term preservation of all object information in the database is guaranteed by a Tivoli Storage System with multiple redundancies. In the last version “Arachne 3” the user interface was based on PHP / JavaScript and Apache with solr for indexing the data. The current version “Arachne 4” introduced a conceptual separation between the frontend in the user’s internet browser and the backend acting as the intermediary between the frontend and the database. The frontend is an AngularJS application, the backend is programmed in Java with elasticsearch instead of solr for indexing.

Database evolution

Arachne started in 1995 as a FileMaker solution. Since 2001 it benefits from the creation of a chair of Digital Humanities (Historisch-Kulturwissenschaftliche Informationsverarbeitung) at the University of Cologne. Its students use Arachne as a test environment for serious and realistic programming projects. Also since 2001, Arachne has integrated archives of image negatives thanks to significant and continuous support from the German Research Foundation (DFG). For example, the archives of the photographers Barbara Malter and Gisela Fittschen-Badura were digitized and documented. Starting in 2003, the sculpture negatives of the DAI in Rome were also digitized and scientifically documented. This added 40,000 high-quality digital images of ancient sculptures to the collection.

Since 2004 Arachne is operated by a consortium of the DAI and the CoDArchLab. In the same year the technology and semantics of the database together with the data ingestion workflow were completely restructured. The FileMaker data was exported to a MAMP system (“Arachne 2”). With Arachne’s strategic positioning as the central object and image database of an institution that houses some two million images in its archives and constantly produces new object and image data, one can imagine the amount of development and data ingestion work that is still needed to integrate the DAI archives. For example, starting in 2006 the glass negatives of the DAI were digitized in several stages in the DFG-supported Emagines project. At the end of the project, some 150,000 Scans and 60,000 object datasets had been integrated into Arachne.

In 2008/09 Arachne's user interface was completely redesigned (“Arachne 3”). As a partner of the CLAROSnet project, a multilingual interface was created for Arachne in 2009 via a so-called i18n framework. It forms the basis for the localisation to different languages.

In 2009 and 2012 a complete catalogue of the sculptures in the antiquities collection of the Berlin State Museums was created as part of the Berlin Sculpture-Network project and made available in Arachne in 2013. It comprises newly edited scientific catalogue texts, images and archival materials about 2,600 sculptures. The sculptures are supplemented by 3,500 plaster casts from the Cast Collection of Ancient Sculpture in Berlin, which are connected with the datasets of the originals.

Another major step was the DFG-supported project Reception of Antiquity in a Semantic Network that ran from 2009 until 2013. Some 2,300 out-of-copyright printed engravings were digitized and presented in a TEI viewer with their OCR texts. Subsequently, a string of smaller projects added even more digitized books and archive materials. The total portfolio is presented in the iDAI.bookbrowser, which enables direct connections between Arachne objects from the “real world” and their textual descriptions in the digitized books. The iDAI.bookbrowser supports the METS metadata standard via its OAI PMH interface and is connected with external portals such as Propylaeum and ZVDD.

Further information about these and other projects can be found here.

After a beta phase in 2016, “Arachne 4” became the standard version in 2017 (see also the section Technology).

Status: facts and numbers

As of November 2016, there are 17,235 registered users with 6,000 of these users from outside of Germany. The database currently comprises about 3.8 million entries.

  • 2.4 million images
  • more than 250,000 contextualized objects
  • 6,400 books with 1.1 million book pages
